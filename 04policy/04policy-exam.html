1.1. What is the role of risk management in the formulation of IA policy?

Performing risk management adds clarity and detailed guidance for information asset protection. Risk assessment is used to identify critical assets, identify threats to those assets, and formulate asset protection strategies based on identified risks. Protecting critical information assets is one of the primary purposes of IA policy. This is addressed in Principle 3: Not all data is of equal value - risk must be managed.

1.2. Who needs to participate in policy formulation and enforcement?

Participants include senior managers (CEO, CIO), middle managers including the IT manager, legal counsel, human resources, security staff, administrators and other IT staff, affected users, and affected third parties (contractors, vendors, suppliers, partners).

1.3. What are three characteristics of an effective IA policy?

  1. Realistic-balances protection with productivity

  2. Visible and actively enforced with demonstrated senior management sponsorship

  3. Accompanied by regular awareness and training sessions

1.4. What topics might you expect to see covered in a general security policy?

  1. Staff roles and responsibilities including system administrators

  2. Password policy and protection

  3. Network connectivity and access (internal, external, Internet), and monitoring

  4. System access control privileges including special administrator privileges

  5. Information backup

  6. Contingency planning and disaster recovery

  7. Physical security

1.5. What topics might you expect to see covered in an acceptable use policy?

  1. Compliance with the enterprise's password policy

  2. Hardware changes a user may or may not make including modem restrictions and use

  3. Software a user may or may not install or remove

  4. Information a user may or may not transmit across the network

  5. User responsibilities to operate a computer securely including virus scanning and eradication

  6. Prohibitions against sharing accounts and conducting personal business

1.6. What questions should users be able to answer about their enterprise's IA policy?

  1. Where are your enterprise's IA policies defined?

  2. Who is involved in establishing IA policy?

  3. Who is responsible for monitoring IA policy compliance?

  4. How do you acknowledge you have received and read your IA policies and all applicable updates?

1.7. What three examples of policy topics that are required to protect administrators?

  1. Authority and conditions for monitoring user activities (e.g., reading user e-mail)

  2. Disrupting or terminating service under specific conditions

  3. Adding and changing users, user groups, user privileges, and authentication mechanisms

  4. Authority and conditions for using vulnerability testing, penetration testing, and password cracking tools

  5. Enforcing acceptable use policy provisions and prohibitions

1.8. What is an administrator's role in IA policy formulation and enforcement?

  1. Content contributor for procedures, processes, and tools

  2. Enforcer in areas of responsibility

  3. Trainer
